I had already made up my mind. I just hadn't pulled the trigger yet.

It was a Thursday morning in Bentonville, Arkansas — my last day at Walmart's Intelligent Retail Lab. I was burnt out, anxious, and quietly grieving a version of myself that had stopped caring about the work months ago. The late nights, the pressure, the constant performance reviews — it had all started bleeding into my sleep. I couldn't remember the last time I felt genuinely motivated.

I called an Uber to the office one final time.

Bentonville is a strange place. It's a city curated and controlled by the largest retailer in the world. It's also a manufactured environment where people from across the country are relocated and thrown together into a cultural melting pot that never really fuses. Growing infrastructure had brought a surge of new residents, each with their own story of why they ended up there. I had become a character in that story too, except I never stopped feeling like I didn't belong.

My driver that morning was a grandpa who had recently moved from Omaha, Nebraska. I asked him what brought him out here.

He told me the cost of living back home had made it difficult to afford his granddaughter's medication. She had been diagnosed with a severe form of muscular dystrophy: a condition that limited her movement and restricted her to crutches. He moved to Bentonville for work. Not because he wanted to. Because he had to.

Something about that hit differently than I expected.

I grew up with Pyruvate Kinase Deficiency, a rare genetic disease that altered the lifespan of my red blood cells and caused chronic hemolytic anemia. As a child, I spent more time in hospital beds than on playgrounds. I knew what it felt like to have a body that worked against you. And I knew, from watching my own parents, what it cost a family emotionally to watch a child struggle with something no one could fix.

I told him about my hospital visits. About growing up with a condition that made me feel physically inferior to everyone around me. About finding computer science not as a career path, but as an escape — a place where my limitations didn't follow me. And about how somewhere along the way, I had drifted from that origin. I had traded purpose for stability, and it was slowly hollowing me out.

The crossroads I had been circling for months suddenly felt very clear.

On one side: stay at Walmart. Keep the six-figure salary. Absorb the late nights and the anxiety and the growing sense that none of it meant anything. On the other: walk away. Move to Washington D.C. Take a pay cut. Join the National Institutes of Health as a research fellow and work at the intersection of computation and human biology, exactly where I had always wanted to be.

Less money. Higher expenses. No guarantee I'd even be good at it.

But when he pulled up to the office and put the car in park, he turned to look at me. He shook my hand and told me to take the risk no one else was willing to take.

I quit the next day. I never looked back.

I'm now at the NIH, working alongside some of the sharpest minds in the world and people who have dedicated their careers to understanding and treating the rarest diseases in existence. Every day I think about what it would have meant to have more research, more tools, more urgency around conditions like mine when I was a kid. That thought doesn't leave me.

I'm writing this because I know I'm not the only one who has sat at that crossroads. The one where comfort fights meaning, and neither side feels entirely safe. If you're there right now — uncertain, burnt out, quietly grieving a version of yourself you used to recognize — I hope this gives you something.

Sometimes the clearest answer comes from the most unexpected place.

For me, it came from a grandpa in an Uber who had his own reasons to keep going.
