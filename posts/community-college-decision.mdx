I graduated high school at 17 as my class valedictorian. For four years I had run on almost no sleep, up until 2 AM studying for calculus exams, awake again at 6, doing extra problems on weekends just to stay ahead. It was an obsession. Grades mattered more than food, sleep, or my own sanity. I was trapped in a bubble where the only thing that counted was getting into the best school possible.

Then the acceptance letters came. I got into every UC I applied to. That high lasted about ten minutes, until I opened the financial aid packages.

I was a first-generation college student. Even with generous aid, the cost of living in a California college city made the numbers impossible without taking on serious debt, and I wasn't willing to start my life that way. Which left one option: my local community college, the same campus I had been taking classes at since 8th grade. I knew exactly what it looked like. That familiarity made it worse.

I told myself I was too good for it. I was wrong, and I was too self-absorbed to see what I was actually being handed.

The moment I walked onto that campus as a full-time student, something shifted. My classmates weren't slackers or fallbacks. They were parents juggling children and full-time jobs, adults going back to finish something they'd started years ago, people betting on themselves with a lot more on the line than I had ever risked. I had nothing on them.

I had spent four years believing that ambition looked like perfect grades and elite admissions. Walking those hallways, I realized I had confused ambition with ego. These people were more ambitious than I had ever been. They just didn't have the luxury of performing it.

Education is a blessing. I had been treating it like a competition.

Community college changed me in ways I didn't expect. I stopped chasing grades and started chasing experiences. Without the weight of external expectations, I had space to actually explore, to build things because I was curious, to read about problems that kept me up at night for reasons that had nothing to do with an exam. I taught myself how to code not for a class but because I wanted to understand how things worked. That curiosity pulled me toward biology, then toward the intersection of computation and science, and eventually toward questions about life itself: how proteins fold, how organisms adapt, how we might one day engineer solutions to problems that feel impossibly large. Community college didn't constrain that path. It created it. The version of me that went straight to a four-year university would have been too busy performing to ever start becoming.
